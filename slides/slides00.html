Map Power
"More indigenous territory has been claimed by maps than by guns. This assertion has its corollary: more indigenous territory can be reclaimed and defended by maps than by guns."

"Whereas maps like guns must be accurate, they have the additional advantages that they are inexpensive, don't require a permit, can be openly carried and used, internationally neutralize the invader's one-sided legalistic claims, and can be duplicated and transmitted electronically which defies all borders, all pretexts, and all occupations."

Why Make Maps (apart from navigation)

Tool of Statecraft

as far back as Rome
  • Divide conquered land
  • Reclaim appropriated state lands
  • State revenue (taxes)
    late 16th and 17th C
    rise of capitalist social relations
  • Survey (governance)

Estate map

late 13th or 14th C onwards
  • Profit
    precision, permanence, governance and management of natural resources
  • Describe/plot boundaries
  • Resolve/avoid disputes
    tenants, landlords, and between landlords
  • Legal security

  • Process is more important than product
  • ¿Participatory data gathering?

Problems in Participatory / Indigenous Mapping
  • Who is empowered and who is dis-empowered
  • Non-commensurable epistemologies between knowledge systems
  • Erasure through the use of GIS and western cartography
  • Forced use of hard boundaries where there may be none
  • Exacerbated power differentials locally and at larger societal scales
  • Possibilities of generating conflict with maps
  • Results will not come quickly
  • Maps do not exist outside of political contexts
  • Always a hybrid product both in terms of process and product
Norris, Timothy. (2014). Bridging the Great Divide: state, civil society, and 'participatory' conservation mapping in a resource extraction zone. Applied Geography, 54(2014), 262-274. doi: 10.1016/j.apgeog.2014.05.016

Why make maps ...

"The main purpose of the maps described here is to document and establish boundaries between forest villagers and external claimants, from the local point of view, and to re-claim for local people some of the territory being appropriated by state and international forest mapping projects. Local notions of territoriality have had to change as extensive land-based projects have threatened them; they will change further with mapping. Yet, given the alternate futures - of not being on the map, as it were, being obscured from view and having local claims obscured, there almost seems to be no choice. (pp 402-403, emphasis mine)

Towards a Conclusion
  • Interesting relationships between Indigenous peoples, conservation actors, academic activists, and extractive industries
  • Hybrid nature of anything that comes out of these relationships
  • Ownership of paper maps is shifting to OCAP of data - Indigenous data governance
  • Anti-colonial and de-colonial mapping exist together, neither more important
  • Constitutional land rights are a key when using maps to defend rights
  • Participatory mapping remains within the colonial project
  • Ongoing importance of other ways of knowing, perhaps more important than maps
